# Venture Lenses — Disclosed Reference

The council of lenses for venture analysis. Each lens examines the idea from one archetype's domain. Apply in sequence after the core rules (1-5) in the main skill have been completed.

These are not optional — every lens must be applied for a complete analysis.

---

# Lens 1: Narada Market Signal

Narada Lens asks:

"What is the market already saying?"

Search for:

* Customer complaints
* Competitor reviews
* "alternative to X"
* "X pricing"
* "X vs Y"
* "best tools for X"
* "problems with X"
* "how to solve X manually"
* "X template"
* "X Excel sheet"
* "X automation"
* "X open source"
* "X integration"
* "X enterprise"

Look for:

* Repeated complaints
* Repeated buying intent
* Repeated workaround
* Repeated dissatisfaction
* Expensive tools with poor satisfaction
* Strong demand but weak solutions
* Niche vertical need ignored by big players

Market signals are stronger when they appear in many independent places.

Output:

* Signal strength (strong / moderate / weak / absent)
* Primary signal sources
* Demand patterns identified
* Unmet needs visible in market

# Lens 2: Map Direct, Indirect, and Hidden Competition

Competition is not only another startup.

Ask:

* Who solves this directly?
* Who solves part of this?
* What manual workaround exists?
* What internal tool do companies already use?
* What spreadsheet/report/process replaces this?
* What open-source tool exists?
* What consulting company solves this?
* What platform feature already exists?
* What large vendor can add this easily?
* What happens if the customer does nothing?

Competition map:

* Direct competitors
* Indirect competitors
* Substitutes
* Manual workarounds
* Internal tools
* Open-source alternatives
* Platform features
* Service providers
* "Do nothing" option

If there is no competition, be careful.

It may mean there is no market.

Output:

* Competition intensity
* Strongest competitor and their weakness
* Gap in competitive landscape
* Customer switching cost from current solution
* "Do nothing" viability

# Lens 3: Incumbent Kill Test

This is one of the most important tests.

Ask:

"If a large existing company adds this feature, do we still survive?"

Ask:

* Can Microsoft add this?
* Can Google add this?
* Can AWS add this?
* Can Salesforce add this?
* Can ServiceNow add this?
* Can Zoho add this?
* Can HubSpot add this?
* Can Datadog add this?
* Can Snowflake add this?
* Can an existing vertical SaaS add this?
* Can an SI/consulting company deliver this as a service?
* Can an open-source repo become good enough?

Then ask:

* What remains unique about us?
* Do we have domain depth?
* Do we have proprietary workflow knowledge?
* Do we own data access?
* Do we have faster service delivery?
* Do we have vertical specialization?
* Do we have trust with a buyer segment?
* Do we have implementation skill competitors lack?
* Do we have a wedge big companies ignore?

If a big company can add this as a small checkbox feature, we must not build a generic version.

We need a sharper wedge.

Output:

* Incumbent threat level (high / medium / low)
* Most dangerous incumbent
* What remains defensible
* Survival verdict: Survives / Weak / Dies

# Lens 4: Feature or Company

Many ideas are features, not companies.

Ask:

* Is this valuable enough to buy separately?
* Is this only useful inside another platform?
* Will customers pay for this alone?
* Does this require owning a larger workflow?
* Can this expand into a bigger product?
* Is this a wedge into a bigger system?
* Would a platform vendor treat this as a feature?
* Can we build a business around this, or only a demo?

Verdict options:

* Standalone product
* Feature inside existing product
* Service/POC offering
* Wedge into bigger platform
* Internal tool only
* Not worth building

Do not mistake a useful feature for a venture.

Output:

* Classification (standalone product / feature / wedge / service)
* Expansion potential
* Revenue ceiling if feature-only
* Path to standalone viability (if any)

# Lens 5: Chanakya Competitive Strategy

Chanakya Lens asks:

"How do we enter the market and survive?"

Ask:

* What is our wedge?
* What beachhead segment should we start with?
* Which niche is painful but underserved?
* Which competitor is weak in this niche?
* What buyer is easiest to reach?
* What use case gives fastest proof?
* What segment has budget and urgency?
* What positioning makes us different?
* What partnership can help entry?
* What must we avoid fighting directly?

Output:

* Beachhead customer
* Wedge use case
* Entry strategy
* Positioning statement
* Differentiation
* First proof point
* Expansion path

Do not enter the strongest gate.

Enter through the weakest wall.

# Lens 6: Kubera Economics

Kubera Lens asks:

"Will wealth flow back?"

Ask:

* Who pays?
* How much will they pay?
* Is the budget existing or new?
* Is this CapEx, OpEx, SaaS, consulting, or success-fee?
* What is customer acquisition cost?
* What is sales cycle length?
* What is gross margin?
* What is delivery cost?
* What is support cost?
* What is infrastructure cost?
* What is LLM/API cost?
* What is integration cost?
* What is payback period?
* What is churn risk?
* Can this become profitable without endless custom service?

Output:

* Pricing hypothesis
* Revenue model
* Cost structure
* Margin risk
* Payback estimate
* Unit economics risk
* Monetization verdict

If money does not return, the idea is not a business.

It is an expense.

# Lens 7: Distribution

A good product can fail if buyers cannot be reached.

Ask:

* How will we reach buyers?
* Do we already have access?
* Is this sold through founder-led sales?
* Is this sold through partners?
* Is this product-led?
* Is this enterprise sales?
* Is this content-led?
* Is this community-led?
* Is this marketplace-led?
* Is this outbound-led?
* How long will trust-building take?
* What proof does the buyer need before responding?

Output:

* Primary channel
* Secondary channel
* Sales motion
* Distribution risk
* Trust-building requirement
* First 10 customer path

Distribution is not marketing decoration.

Distribution is survival.

# Lens 8: Vishwakarma Feasibility

Can this be built, integrated, deployed, and maintained?

Ask:

* What data is needed?
* Where will the data come from?
* Is the data accessible?
* Is the data clean enough?
* What integrations are needed?
* What systems must connect?
* What AI/LLM capability is required?
* What cannot be automated reliably?
* What human-in-the-loop is needed?
* What architecture is needed for MVP?
* What is risky to build now?
* What is expensive to operate?
* What will break at scale?

Output:

* Technical feasibility
* Data feasibility
* Integration feasibility
* AI feasibility
* Operational feasibility
* MVP complexity
* Build risk

A feasible demo is not the same as a feasible product.

# Lens 9: Data Reality

Many AI ideas fail because data is missing, messy, delayed, inaccessible, or politically blocked.

Ask:

* Does the customer have the data?
* Who owns the data?
* Is the data structured?
* Is the data updated regularly?
* Is historical data available?
* Is data quality good enough?
* Are labels available?
* Can we access production data?
* Is sample data representative?
* Can we legally process it?
* Can we get it without long IT dependency?
* What happens if data is incomplete?

Output:

* Data availability
* Data quality
* Data owner
* Data access method
* Data latency
* Data risk
* Required data contract

No data, no AI product.

Bad data, bad product.

# Lens 10: Yama Compliance

Some ideas fail because the boundary is not technical.

It is legal, privacy, security, compliance, procurement, or ethics.

Ask:

* Are we handling sensitive data?
* Are we handling financial data?
* Are we handling health data?
* Are we handling employee data?
* Are we handling customer PII?
* Are there data residency requirements?
* Are there audit requirements?
* Are there industry regulations?
* Is procurement difficult?
* Is security review heavy?
* Could this create legal liability?
* Could this create ethical harm?

Output:

* Legal risk
* Security risk
* Privacy risk
* Procurement risk
* Ethical risk
* Boundary conditions
* Required controls

If Yama says the boundary is hard, do not ignore it.

Hard boundaries break careless ventures.

# Lens 11: Gargi Question Challenge

Gargi Lens asks:

"Are we asking the wrong question?"

Ask:

* Is the problem framed correctly?
* Are we solving a symptom instead of the root cause?
* Is this idea based on a false comparison?
* What assumption is hidden in the question?
* What would disprove the idea?
* What question are we avoiding?
* What would a smart skeptic ask?
* Is there a simpler non-product solution?
* Are we building technology because we like technology?

Output:

* Reframed problem
* Hidden assumptions
* Disconfirming evidence
* Better question
* Alternative solution path

A bad question creates a bad product even if execution is good.

# Lens 12: Shakuni Attack

Shakuni Lens asks:

"What hidden assumption kills this?"

Ask:

* What must be true for this idea to work?
* What if that belief is false?
* What dependency are we ignoring?
* What buyer behavior are we assuming?
* What user behavior are we assuming?
* What integration risk are we ignoring?
* What competitor response are we underestimating?
* What cost are we hiding?
* What will not scale?
* What will customers misuse?
* What will make the demo look good but product fail?

Output:

* Kill assumption
* Failure path
* Hidden dependency
* Misuse path
* Scaling risk
* Overconfidence warning

Do not test the idea where it looks strong.

Attack the belief that makes it look obvious.

# Lens 13: Shukracharya Competitor Response

Shukracharya Lens asks:

"How will the opponent fight?"

Ask:

* What will competitors copy?
* What will competitors bundle for free?
* What will competitors undercut?
* What will competitors say against us?
* What will incumbents use as unfair advantage?
* Can they use distribution against us?
* Can they use existing contracts against us?
* Can they use data access against us?
* Can they use trust/security/compliance against us?
* Can they acquire a smaller competitor?
* Can they block integrations?

Output:

* Expected competitor moves
* Incumbent advantage
* Our vulnerability
* Defensive positioning
* Counter-strategy

If the opponent can kill us with one bundling decision, we need a different wedge.

# Lens 14: Right-to-Win

A market can be real and still not be ours.

Ask:

* Why us?
* Why now?
* Why would customers trust us?
* What do we know that others do not?
* What relationship do we have?
* What capability do we have?
* What speed advantage do we have?
* What domain expertise do we have?
* What data access do we have?
* What distribution path do we have?
* What proof can we show?
* What can we do that big companies will not bother doing?

Output:

* Right-to-win reason
* Weakness in right-to-win
* Required proof
* Required partner/customer
* Differentiation strength

If there is no right-to-win, the idea may still be good but not for us.

# Lens 15: Moat or Wedge

Do not claim "AI" as moat.

AI is not enough.

Ask:

* What is hard to copy?
* Is there proprietary data?
* Is there workflow depth?
* Is there domain specialization?
* Is there distribution advantage?
* Is there integration advantage?
* Is there switching cost?
* Is there trust advantage?
* Is there implementation expertise?
* Is there regulatory/compliance depth?
* Is there community or ecosystem?
* Is there a service-to-product learning loop?

Moat types:

* Data moat
* Workflow moat
* Distribution moat
* Trust moat
* Domain moat
* Integration moat
* Service-learning moat
* Switching-cost moat

A weak moat means the idea must enter through a sharp wedge.

Output:

* Moat type identified (or none)
* Moat strength (strong / developing / weak / absent)
* Wedge strategy if moat is weak
* Time to defensibility

---

# Lens 16: Tarpit Detection

Some ideas always look attractive but repeatedly trap founders.

Ask:

* Have many startups tried this before?
* Why did they fail?
* Is customer behavior harder than it looks?
* Is willingness to pay weak?
* Is acquisition too expensive?
* Is the product too easy to copy?
* Is integration too painful?
* Is the buyer too slow?
* Is the market full of "almost useful" tools?
* Does everyone say yes but nobody pays?

Output:

* Tarpit risk (high / medium / low)
* Historical failure pattern
* What must be different this time
* Evidence needed to proceed

A tarpit idea is not always impossible.

But it demands stronger proof than normal.
